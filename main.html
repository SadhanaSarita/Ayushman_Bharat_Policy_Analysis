🔍 Key Finding — SECC Data Problem

The scheme was designed around the Socio-Economic Caste Census (SECC) of 2011. In 14 years, India's poverty landscape has shifted dramatically. Lakhs of ineligible beneficiaries remain enrolled while genuinely poor households formed after 2011 have no pathway to join, unless their state adopts alternative databases (which only some have done).

🔍 Key Finding — The "Missing Middle"

AB-PMJAY serves the very poor (SECC-listed). The middle class has private insurance or none. But a huge segment — informal workers just above the poverty line, urban migrants, gig economy workers — fall in the "missing middle": too rich to qualify, too poor to afford private insurance. The scheme does not address this group at all.

🔍 Key Finding — State Funding Disputes

The Centre funds 60% and states 40% in most cases — but this ratio is disputed. Karnataka alleged it was bearing 75% of costs (Jan 2025), while the Centre claimed 60%. Beneficiaries are routinely caught in Centre–State disputes, with hospitals refusing AB-PMJAY cards citing non-receipt of government instructions or delayed reimbursements.

India's Comptroller and Auditor General released the first-ever performance audit of PMJAY in August 2023, tabling it in Parliament. It found systemic fraud, data corruption, and governance failure across multiple states. The key findings are summarised below.

🔍 Systemic Finding — No Uniform Threshold for Beneficiary Approval

Out of 94.88 lakh rejected beneficiary applications, 38.57 lakh (40.65%) were rejected despite having a match confidence score of 51–100 — meaning they should have been approved. The CAG found that the NHA had not set any objective, uniform threshold for approval or rejection, leaving the process arbitrary and prone to both denial and fraud.

🔍 Systemic Finding — Technology Failure

The government claims AI and ML are used to detect fraud. Yet the CAG audit found the IT system had no mechanisms to prevent simultaneous hospitalisations, no validation controls to catch invalid dates of birth, unrealistic family sizes (up to 2,000+ members in a household), or duplicate Aadhaar registrations. The digital backbone of the scheme had fundamental integrity gaps.

🔍 The Inpatient Bias Problem

PMJAY is entirely focused on hospitalisation. Yet evidence globally shows that preventive care and outpatient management dramatically reduce the need for expensive hospitalisation. By funding only the most expensive end of care, the scheme may be creating perverse incentives for unnecessary admissions while ignoring the upstream healthcare that would reduce hospitalisation costs.

🔍 Private Hospital Dominance

42.4% of empanelled hospitals are private (12,705 hospitals). Multiple reports indicate private hospitals upcoded procedures, performed unnecessary surgeries (particularly for conditions like hysterectomies, cataracts) to maximise reimbursements. This is a documented global problem with insurance-linked schemes.

🔍 Awareness = Access Gap

Studies in Uttar Pradesh (Gautam Buddha Nagar) and other states show that ignorance about the scheme is the primary reason for low utilisation — not lack of cards. In urban areas especially, many eligible families don't know what conditions are covered, which hospitals are empanelled, or how to access care. IEC (Information, Education, Communication) cells were formed in only 7 of audited states.

05
Policy Analysis
What Needs to Change — Evidence-Based Recommendations
01

Update Beneficiary Database From SECC 2011

The scheme must migrate from 14-year-old data to a dynamic, real-time poverty identification system linked to existing databases (NFSA, PM-KISAN, Jan Dhan). A living registry would include newly poor households and exclude those who have escaped poverty, drastically improving targeting efficiency. Pilot models from Andhra Pradesh's "Aarogyasri" scheme show this is achievable.

02

Extend Coverage to Outpatient Care & Medicines

As long as PMJAY covers only hospitalisation, out-of-pocket expenditure will remain catastrophic for the poor. A phased expansion to include generic medicine access (via Jan Aushadhi stores), outpatient diagnostics, and chronic disease management (diabetes, hypertension) would address the most common drivers of medical impoverishment.

03

Fix the IT System — Real Anti-Fraud Controls

The CAG found the technology backbone had no mechanisms to flag simultaneous hospitalisations, dead-patient claims, or duplicate Aadhaar registrations. A minimum requirement: real-time deduplication, biometric verification at admission (not just during card issuance), and automated flagging of statistical outliers in hospital billing patterns.

04

Dramatically Increase the Budget

The 2025 budget of ₹9,406 crore is approximately 18× lower than what would be required to actually provide ₹5 lakh coverage to the claimed 50 crore beneficiaries. Either the coverage promise must be honest about its limits, or the budget must increase substantially. The current arrangement creates an unfunded mandate that states and beneficiaries absorb in hidden costs.

05

Resolve Centre–State Funding Disputes

Beneficiaries cannot be collateral damage in political disputes about fund-sharing ratios. A binding, transparent formula for Centre–State cost-sharing — with automatic release of funds on a pre-defined schedule — would ensure continuity of care and reduce hospital refusals. An independent ombudsman mechanism for beneficiary grievances is also needed.

06

Integrate Primary and Secondary Care

The Health and Wellness Centre (HWC) component of Ayushman Bharat has been severely underfunded and poorly integrated with the PMJAY insurance arm. A proper referral pathway — from HWC to PMJAY-empanelled hospitals — would reduce unnecessary hospitalisation, improve early detection, and reduce long-term costs while improving outcomes.

07

Mandate Independent Third-Party Outcome Evaluation

Currently, most evaluation data comes from government sources or scheme-funded reports. An independent, longitudinal study tracking actual health outcomes (not just claims data) for PMJAY beneficiaries vs matched controls would generate credible evidence on whether the scheme is genuinely improving population health — the only metric that ultimately matters.
